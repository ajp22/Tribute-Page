One of the Founding Fathers of the United States

Benjamin Franklin (January 17, 1706 â€“ April 17, 1790) was a British American polymath and one of the Founding Fathers of the United States. Franklin was a leading writer, printer, political philosopher, politician, Freemason, postmaster, scientist, inventor, humorist, civic activist, statesman, and diplomat. As a scientist, he was a major figure in the American Enlightenment and the history of physics for his discoveries and theories regarding electricity. As an inventor, he is known for the lightning rod, bifocals, and the Franklin stove, among other inventions. He founded many civic organizations, including the Library Company, Philadelphia's first fire department, and the University of Pennsylvania.

Milestones

  1. He is born, in Boston, and baptized in the Old South Church.
  2. At the age of eight, enters the Grammar School.
  3. Writes ballads and peddles them, in printed form, in the streets; contributes, anonymously, to the "New England Courant," and temporarily edits that paper; becomes a free-thinker, and a vegetarian.
  4. Becomes proprietor and editor of the "Pennsylvania Gazette"; prints, anonymously, "Nature and Necessity of a Paper Currency"; opens a stationer's shop.
  5. Marries Rebecca Read
  6. Founds the Philadelphia Library.
  7. Begins to study French, Italian, Spanish, and Latin.
    8. Elected to the Assembly; appointed Deputy Postmaster-General; plans a city police.
  8. Invents the open, or "Franklin," stove.
  9. Experiments with a kite and discovers that lightning is an electrical discharge.
